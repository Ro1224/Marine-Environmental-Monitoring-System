1. Using Oil Booms

The use of oil booms is a straightforward and popular method of controlling oil spills. Equipment called containment booms acts like a fence to prevent the oil from further spreading or floating away. Booms float on the water surface and have three parts:

  • A ‘freeboard’ is the part that rises above the water surface, containing the oil and preventing it from splashing over the top.
  • A ‘skirt’ is placed below the surface and keeps the oil from being squeezed under the booms and escaping.
  • A kind of cable or chain that connects the parts to strengthen and stabilize the boom.

Additional Information: Effective only in calm conditions and for fresh spills.

2. Using Skimmers

Skimmers are devices deployed on boats to collect oil from the surface. Acting like a vacuum cleaner, skimmers recover oil efficiently.

Additional Information: Effective in calm waters but susceptible to clogging by debris.

3. Using Sorbents

Sorbents soak up liquids, including oil, using materials like straw or peat moss. They are commonly used for small spills.

Additional Information: Can become heavy after absorption, requiring careful retrieval.

4. Using Dispersants

Dispersants are chemicals sprayed on oil slicks to break the oil into tiny droplets, which bacteria can naturally degrade.

Additional Information: Effective but may harm aquatic life due to chemical toxicity.

5. In Situ Burning

This method involves burning oil directly on the water surface. It is efficient for quick removal of large spills.

Additional Information: Produces toxic fumes and requires controlled conditions.

6. Bioremediation

This technique uses microorganisms to break down oil into less harmful substances, speeding up natural degradation.

Additional Information: Requires specific environmental conditions to be effective.

7. Manual Cleanup

Manual cleanup uses manpower to remove oil, especially on shorelines or hard-to-reach areas.

Additional Information: Labor-intensive and time-consuming.

8. Use of Vacuum Trucks

Vacuum trucks suck oil directly from water surfaces, efficiently collecting large quantities of oil.

Additional Information: Ideal for larger spills but challenging to use in shallow waters.

9. Use of Absorbent Pads

Absorbent pads are highly effective for soaking up small quantities of oil, often used in shallow waters or near shorelines.

Additional Information: Best suited for final cleanup stages.

10. Chemical Solidifiers

Solidifiers turn oil into a rubber-like solid, which can then be removed manually or mechanically.

Additional Information: Effective in calm waters but may be costly for large spills.
